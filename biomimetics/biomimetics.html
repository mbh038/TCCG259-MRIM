Inspiration from deep-sea species

25,000 species already recorded in World Register of Deep-Sea Species (WoRDSS), (Mora et al. 2011). Many more likely to be found.

Life in the deep sea is specially adapted to thrive in areas characterized by

  • high pressure
  • absence of sunlight
  • limited nutrients.

Examples include

  1. the mirrored eyes of mesopelagic teleost fish

Deep-sea fish vision: the brownsnout spookfish (Dolichopteryx longipes) and glasshead barreleye fish (Rhynchohyalus natalensis) are the two best studied.

No light extends beyond 1,000 m into the ocean, but some faint traces of residual daylight persist at depths from 500 to 1000 m

A range of species in this zone produce bioluminescence (Land 2009) creating a unique sensory environment. This in turn is associated with the double eye strcuture often found in species in the depth range 200-1000m: a large eye pointing upwards and a smaller eye pointing downward. Land (Land 2000) describes how this double eye phenomenon, found across three phyla (vertebrates, crustaceans and molluscs) must imply different functions for the two eyes, since the downwelling light is some 200 times. more intense than the upwelling light from the depths. He argues that the upward pointing eye is diesigned to spot the sillhouette of sinking food, and so needs high resolution, while the downward pointing eye is designed to detect flashes of bioluminesence against a dark background. These flashes may come from potential predators. it is sufficient to know of their presence but unnecessary to know their precise location, hence the downward facing eye can have poor resolution and hence be smaller than the upward facing eye.

A number of mesopelagic teleost fishes have evolved unique eyes that benefit from both sources of light (through a combination of reflective and refractive optics)

The two most-studied of these species are the brownsnout spookfish (Dolichopteryx longipes) and the glasshead barreleye fish (Rhynchohyalus natalensis)

The reflective eyes of decapods like lobsters spurred advances in the field of astrophysics, namely the development of “lobster-eye” X-ray telescopes (248, 249), and the biomimetic design potential of further telescope innovations focused on the unique tilting of mirror plates in D. longipes eyes has been suggested (250, 251).

A recent study created advanced models to test the optical performance of D. longipes, paving the way to the development of optical systems that can function in harsh environments including the deep sea (252).

Other examples of biomimetic design are found in architecture, where the mirrored eyes of D. longipes inspired concepts of energy-saving roof designs by making optimal use of available daylight (253, 254), as well as in the design of a streamlined passenger car based on the body line of R. natalensis (255).
